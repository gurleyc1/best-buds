The idea

A game about
the two of us.

I wanted to make something Lillian and I could play together — not a game downloaded from a store, but one we built ourselves. A neighborhood where both of us exist as characters, with our names, our hair, our outfits.

We started with a small hub world and kept adding things she asked for: a playground with every piece of equipment, an ice cream shop, penalty kicks, tennis rallies. Every feature was Lillian's idea tested on Lillian first.

It runs in a browser, on a phone, anywhere. No install required. Just open it and play.

Meet us

The best buds
in real life.

Chris is a Software Development Director at Oracle. Lillian is a second grader in Orlando, Florida. They have always been, and always will be, Best Buds.

Every feature in this game came from a conversation between the two of them. Lillian tested every build, named every location, and approved every design. In her words: "My dad always makes me laugh, and he is always there for me."

How it happened

Two days, start to finish.

Every session started with Lillian playing what was there and telling us what was wrong. That feedback loop was the entire design process.

Day 1 — Morning
The foundation
We built the hub world, character system, and save manager from scratch. A 1280×1280 open neighborhood with grass, roads, and buildings — both of us as characters you can customize. Every graphic is procedurally drawn: no image files, no sprite sheets.
"The world view is extremely overwhelming. The graphics look very trashy with each individual square looking like its own piece of land." — Chris, after the first iteration
Day 1 — Afternoon
Characters, mini-games & music
We designed our characters together — 10 hair styles, 8 clothing options, editable names, 5 skin tones, all procedurally drawn. Then we added the mini-games we play in real life: side-view tennis, penalty kicks. Every music theme and sound effect is generated live via Web Audio API — zero external files.
"Soccer is too hard again in initial stages." — Lillian
Day 1 — Evening
Keepy uppy & the ice cream shop
Balloon physics with wind that builds over time — both of us hitting independently. Then we added our favorite real-life stop: the ice cream shop. Pick a cone or milkshake, choose from 11 flavors, and both characters eat together on screen.
"Can we add cookie dough? That's my favorite." — Lillian
Day 2 — Morning
The playground
Lillian wanted a real playground — monkey bars, slide, climbing wall, zip line, drawbridge. We built a scrollable 1100px-wide scene where your character actually walks up to each piece of equipment before the animation plays.
"Park should have monkey bars, slide, rock climbing wall, zip line, and a drawbridge." — Lillian
Day 2 — Afternoon
Polish, fixes & this page
We iterated on everything Lillian flagged — court visuals, character placement, the goalie swap in soccer, a sleep sequence when you go home. Then we built this page to share it. Start to finish: two days.
"I love my best bud." — Lillian, age 8
Technical

How it's made.

Everything runs in a browser. No backend, no installs, no licensing fees. The entire game — graphics, audio, physics — is generated in code.

Phaser 3
Scene management, arcade physics, camera system, tweens.
TypeScript
Strict mode. Type-safe scene data, character configs, game state.
Vite
Instant HMR during development. Sub-second builds.
Web Audio API
Procedural music and SFX. Zero external audio files.
Pure Graphics
No sprite sheets. Every character and building drawn with Phaser's Graphics API.
